A HISTORY OF
VIOLENCE & CONTROL

2007, Virginia Tech

On April 16, 2007, a gunman commits a spree shooting, comprising of two attacks which resulted in the deaths of 32 people.

2008, Bipartisan NICS Improvement Amendments Act

In January 8, 2008, the Bipartisan NICS Improvement Amendments Act was signed into law. The rule offered to improve electronic recordkeeping for use in background checks which would bar gun ownership by some who have been deemed mentally impaired. On February 28, 2017, President Trump signed legislation overturning a rule to implement this law.

2012, Sandy Hook

The gunman killed 26 people, 20 children and 6 adults, in the deadliest shooting at an elementary, middle or high school in the country's history.

2013, Host of Bills Fail

The Senate took up a host of bills, including one that would have mandated background checks for private sales at gun shows and all Internet sales, exempting most sales between family and friends. Other bills would have banned military-grade weapons, targeted gun trafficking and people who buy guns for others. Republicans, led by Sen. Grassley, backed legislation that would make it easier to carry guns across state lines, but that would also address mental illness, target straw purchasing and illegal firearm trafficking and increase resources to prosecute gun crimes. On April 18, 2013, the Senate rejected each of the bills.

2015, Episcopal Church in Charleston

During a weekly Bible study at the Emanual African Methodist Episcopal Church in Charleston, South Carolina, a white gunman shot and killed nine black people in attendance.

2015, Bill to Eliminate the "Charleston loophole" Fails

Democrats proposed closing what became known as the “Charleston loophole,” which allows federally licensed firearms dealers to proceed with a sale if the FBI doesn’t complete a background check in three days. Because of this “default proceed” rule, the gunman was allowed to purchase his firearm legally. The bill would have eliminated the three-day deadline, however, it never came up for a vote in either the House or Senate.

2015, San Bernardino

A married couple fired automatic weapons at community members at the Inland Regional Center in San Bernardino, California, killing 14 people.

2015, Senate rejects proposal to set up measures to prevent firearm purchases

The Senate revisited the host of bills rejected in 2013, along with different “no-fly, no buy” measures that would prevent or delay individuals on the terror watch list from purchasing firearms. Still, the Senate rejected the measures a day after the shooting.

2016, Pulse Nightclub

A gunman claiming allegiance to the Islamic State killed 49 people and left 53 injured at the Pulse nightclub in Orlando, Florida.

2016, More measures Fail

The Senate finally takes up Grassley’s bill, “no-fly, no buy” legislation, and a bill by Sen. Chris Murphy, D-Conn., requiring universal background checks. The measures fail.

2017, Las Vegas Concert

In an open fire on concertgoers from the 32nd floor of a Las Vegas hotel, a gunman killed 58 people and injured more than 500. This is the deadliest mass shooting in modern American history.

2017, Bump Stock Legislation Proposed

Lawmakers in both parties proposed banning “bump stocks” that accelerate a semi-automatic rifle’s rate of fire. House Speaker Paul Ryan, R-Wis., said regulations are the “smartest, quickest fix" to address rapid-fire devices like the one used in the Las Vegas mass murder; the legislation is still pending.

2017, Sutherland Springs, Texas

During a shooting on November 5th 2017, a gunman opened fire at the First Baptist Church in Sutherland Springs, killing 26 people. This gunman was able to purchase his weapons despite a domestic violence conviction.

2017, GOP talks Bill to fix background checks

After shootings, Republicans have been quick to say that gun control legislation isn’t the answer. But the clerical error that allowed this gunman to buy a gun even when he wasn’t supposed to, has given GOP lawmakers a small window to act on gun control. Senate Majority Whip John Cornyn of Texas announced Tuesday afternoon that he was introducing a bill to try and incentivize federal agencies to put in the system.

2018, Parkland, Florida

On February 14th 2018, a former student turned gunman slaughtered 17 people, 14 being students, at Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School in Prkland, Florida.

2018, Student Activism, the "Red Flag" Bill and Ban on Bump Stocks

Students took the lead pushing for action after the tragedy. But they made little headway in Congress, with Republicans in control of the Senate. Senator Marco Rubio, Republican of Florida, introduced a “red flag” bill, seeking to give law enforcement the ability to restrict gun access for unstable and potentially violent people. The Trump administration issued a new rule banning bump stocks, the attachments that enable semiautomatic rifles to rapid fire.

2019, El Paso and Dayton

A man wearing body armor shot and killed nine people, including his sister, in Dayton, Ohio, hours after a gunman with a rifle entered a Walmart in El Paso and killed 23 people in August 2019.

2019, Failure to Expand Background Checks

Immediately after the shootings, Mr. Trump opened the door to expanded background checks and other proposals to keep guns away from unstable people. However, Mr. Trump backed away from his position, saying that sufficient background checks were in place and the focus should instead be on mental health.

2020, Gun Violence Prevention and Community Safety Act of 2020

The Gun Violence Prevention and Community Safety Act would, among other items, ban the purchasing and possession of assault weapons. The bill addresses background checks, taxes on firearms and goods related to firearms, gun storage, accessibility of guns on school campuses, and more.

2022, Robb Elementary School in Uvalde, Texas

On May 24, 2022, a mass shooting occurred at Robb Elementary School in Uvalde, Texas, United States, where a former student at the school, fatally shot 19 students and two teachers, while 17 others survived despite being injured.

2022, Bipartisan Safer Communities Act and Ban of Military-Style Magazines

The Bipartisan Safer Communities Act was the result of negotiations by a handful of Republican and Democratic senators. The law provides more funding for mental health services and school security, expands criminal background checks for some gun buyers, bars a larger group of domestic-violence offenders from purchasing firearms, and funds Red Flag programs that allow police to seize guns from troubled individuals.
